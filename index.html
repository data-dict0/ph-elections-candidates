The Philippines' shrinking election space

The number of candidates for public office next year has slumped to its lowest since at least 1998

By Prinz Magtulis


Filipinos will troop to elections next year with a limited number of choices for public officials, a shrinking field of candidates that political scientists say, could threaten the country's democratic system.
There are 40,094 candidates for national and local seats in next year's midterm elections, the lowest number of candidacies since the 1998 elections, the earliest data available provided by the Commission on Elections (Comelec).
What's more these numbers are preliminary and poised to decline further as Comelec is expected to get rid of names it deems as nuisance candidates.

In 2001, the earliest midterm elections for which data is available, the average was three candidates each position. The Philippines holds elections every three years for local leaders such as mayors and governors, members of the House of Representatives and 12 of 24 senators. The country elects a president and vice president every six years.
While strictly there is no ideal number of candidates, having a few of them running for a position makes an election less competitive, a critical ingredient in a democratic exercise, Anthony Lawrence Borja, an associate professor of public administration from De La Salle University said.
In extreme cases, Maria Ela Atienza, a political scientist at University of the Philippines-Diliman, said this can happen especially in races where candidates run unopposed. Data requested from the Comelec did not include this information.
"Simply put, if this trend continues, then elections are rendered into mere rubber-stamps for an incumbent's legitimacy," Borja said.

While the quantity of candidates seems bleak, its gender composition may offer some reprieve. Of the total, 8,712 candidates are females, representing 21.7% of the candidates, the largest share since 1998. In absolute terms, the biggest cohort of female candidates at 9,483 was recorded in 2022.
House, Senate diverge
Beyond the data, a narrowing election field couldn't be more obvious. Familiar politicians are gunning for reelection or aiming to return to office after losing the 2022 polls. If not them, their family members from parents to siblings are making their debut, either to replace relatives whose terms are expiring or run for another position, a report by the Philippine Center for Investigative Journalism showed.

The decline in candidacies is deepest at the House of Representatives.

A total of 573 candidates are running to become part of the Lower House next year, down 17% and 8% from 2022 and 2019, respectively, and much lower than 660 candidates two decades ago.

Fewer candidates are running for more seats as the House continuously expanded its membership.

There are now 254 House representatives, up from just 208 in 1998 as more districts get established.

Next year, one new district in South Cotabato will add a new seat at the House. On the flipside, among House members, the number of party-lists running next year declined by over a fifth to 137, data showed.
The opposite is happening at the Senate. There are 127 senatorial candidates next year, nearly double the number from 2022 and the largest in available Comelec data. The appeal of a higher position can be one reason, Atienza said, who was also quick to point to “celebrities” running and winning a Senate seat (in the current Senate, there are at least four former actors namely Robin Padilla, Jinggoy Estrada, Ramon Revilla Jr. and Lito Lapid)

Borja was more nuanced and took note of the trend. For instance, in 2010, the number of senatorial candidates also nearly doubled from 2007 a surge that "was probably due to the unpopularity of the Arroyo administration." "When a barely legitimate regime ends, a vacuum emerges in favor of more prospective candidates seeking to present an alternative to their predecessor," he said.

From 61 in 2010, senatorial candidates dipped to 33 in 2013 before rising again to 50 in 2016 and thereafter.
"What we have from 2016 onwards is a mix of dynasts and outsiders, with the former reflecting old oligarchic defects and the latter embodying a need to bring more supposedly nontraditional politicians into the Senate," he explained, citing the Villars and Binays who currently hold positions at the Senate and local office.
Local races
Races for mayors, governors and their vice are also becoming tighter. The number of gubernatorial candidates, at 230, is down 11% from 2022 and a larger 14.2% from the last midterm polls in 2019, Comelec data showed. Smaller decreases were recorded for vice governors at 15.6% and 0.5%, respectively.
There are 3,647 candidates for mayor and 3,344 for vice mayor, data showed. These candidacies are down 8% and 6.9%, from the 2019 midterm elections, respectively.
While a dwindling pool of choices for public office is not good, having more of them is the necessary fix, Borja said. "Should we even encourage more people to run for office? Electoral politics is a matter of quantity and quality," he said.
